Recylce Upcycle


Overview

Recycle Upcycle was created to introduce the user to the idea of repurposing refuse in order to increase the life span of everyday objects with the end goal of combatting the landfill. Disposable items are ubiquitous and yet a responsible means for disposal are underused or unknown to users. By allowing users to share inspirational ideas on how to give a second life to their unwanted items, Recycle Upcycle navigates the upcycling waters and breathes new life into unwanted items.

Team Size/Role Responsibility

The application was created by myself, Nathaniel Higgins, and Mel Perry. We worked in tandem through the research and design process.

Timeline & Scope

We had an expedited, 3-week timeline to research, design and present this concept. . Our original scope was to increase recycling habits. However, after our initial research we determined that incorporating a repurpose, or “upcycle,” option for items was critical to our success.

Problem Statement

Recycling isn’t always as easy as finding a blue bin. Even the most eco-conscious recyclers are unsure of how to properly recycle everyday items and where to recycle items not meant for curbside recycling. Additionally, some items are better repurposed, or upcycled, rather than being tossed away.

Solution

Provide a tool that educates the user on clear recycling guidelines, locate appropriate recycling locations, and incentivise eco-conscious individuals who want to improve their recycling routine, by rewarding engagement and encouraging users to build a community of like-minded recycling creatives, all in an effort to have an overall significant impact on the environment.

Target Users

Our target users are people between 30 and 60 who are already have a relationship to recycling and want to increase their practice.
Our primary persona, Jen Teller, is a young urban professional who already has a strong relationship with recycling but is seeking better tools to increase her recycling routine.

Our secondary persona, Raina Gaines, is a middle class suburban mom who has a broader concern incorporating eco-conscious activities, but lacks motivation to make it a major priority.

Process

The first step was to explore the market and perform a competitive analysis on existing recycling-geared apps. We broke down other’s competitive edge and weaknesses to inform how we could model our project to meet the users’ needs.
The next step was to conduct a series of interviews with candidates that fit into our target user base. We used the data collected from the interviews to inform and build a digital survey with Google Forms.

These initial processes created the raw data that we used to ideate and iterate. We took the raw data, identified patterns, and noted our significant findings. Then we took these nuggets of data and organized them into an Affinity Diagram.

From here we were able to ideate using the I Like, I Wish, What If method and prioritize these ideas.


We took our findings and distilled what the user deemed important into our main goals:

Educate
  • How to recycle
  • Where to recycle
Engage
  • Improve recycling consistency & routine

Which then informed our three main functions:

  1. Scan to find items
  2. Clear, concise recycling guidelines
  3. Positive Reinforcement - clean up the park, earn points, and unlock other “EnviroScenes” through achievements


The Pivot

During this whole process we were still collecting research. Well into the design phase of this project, we came upon an exposé on American recycling habits which made us reconsider our focus. The article pointed out how China, the leading purchasers or America’s recycled goods, had recently updated their policy on plastics and would no longer accept much of America’s recycled goods which in turn has begun to be shipped and dumped on impoverished nations. This striking discovery forced us to totally reconsider the trajectory of our project. Is recycling doing more harm than good? Can we promote a product that promotes sending our trash overseas? This was certainly an ethical dilemma.
Challenges such as these are a good thing though because they promote creativity and new ideas. We used this recycling revelation to fuel our big pivot to make repurposing unwanted items, or “upcycling,” as a new focus of the app. If recycling certain goods could cause harm, we could have our app promote repurposing and giving unwanted items a second life.

Now we had four main functionality components:

  1. Scan to find items
  2. Clear, concise recycling guidelines
  3. Positive Reinforcement - clean up the park, earn points, and unlock other “EnviroScenes” through achievements
  4. Clear, concise recycling guidelines

Process To Prototype

Then began the rapid sketching of wireframes to inform a final prototype. These initial sketches were then incorporated into a lo-fi clickable prototype for our first round of user testing.


From our first round of user testing with sketched prototypes we came to the following results:

  1. Add “scan” feature to bottom menu nav
  2. Add button to scan
  3. Show points on top of the home screen
  4. Make EnviroPoints one page

The next move was to create an updated clickable prototype using Adobe XD. We used the results from the initial user testing and also referenced our previous work to inform the more crystalized design.

Then began another round of user testing to better our overall design.

Above you can see that we added the Home indicator at the top to better meet iOS standards; we added a Scan button on the bottom navigation; and we included the EnviroPoints on the home page.

Lessons Learned

Our recycling app went in a different direction than originally anticipated. However, we believe that trusting research and continually looping back to various stages of the design process serves to strengthen the overall concept.
We found that the design process is about the evolution of an initial idea. Most importantly, good research cannot be underestimated.

"Design thinking isn't a linear path. It's a big mass of looping back to different places in the process"
David Kelly, Founder IDEO

Future Opportunities

Here is a list of our future iteration ideas:

  • Donations
  • Filter Upcycling projects by room of house
  • Log other environmental activities and tie to rewards points
  • Work with local municipalities to coordinate community recycling efforts
  • Neighborhood events
  • Recycling reminders and notifications
